Normalization Techniques

From BatchNorm to LayerNorm to RMSNorm — why normalization is the unsung hero of stable Transformer training, and how a one-line change (Pre-LN vs Post-LN) revolutionized large-scale language modeling.

Intuition 3.1 — Why Do We Need Normalization?

3.1.1 Why Deep Networks Need Normalization

Imagine a signal passing through a hundred sequential layers. At each layer, the signal gets multiplied by a weight matrix. If the average multiplication factor is even slightly above 1 — say 1.01 — then after 100 layers the signal magnitude is \(1.01^{100} \approx 2.7\). That seems manageable, but with a factor of 1.1 it becomes \(1.1^{100} \approx 13{,}781\). Values explode. Gradients follow suit, and training collapses.

The converse is equally dangerous: if the factor is 0.99, then \(0.99^{100} \approx 0.366\), and with 0.9 it becomes \(0.9^{100} \approx 2.66 \times 10^{-5}\). Activations shrink toward zero; gradients vanish, and the network stops learning.

The Thermostat Analogy. Normalization acts like a thermostat for your network's activations. Without it, the "temperature" of each layer drifts unpredictably — sometimes searing hot (exploding), sometimes freezing cold (vanishing). Normalization pins the temperature to a comfortable range at every layer, ensuring the network can learn stably no matter how deep it is.

In short, normalization prevents the cascading amplification (or attenuation) of signals across layers by re-centering and re-scaling activations to a well-defined range after each transformation step.

3.1.2 The Internal Covariate Shift Argument

When Ioffe & Szegedy introduced Batch Normalization in 2015, they motivated it with the concept of internal covariate shift: each layer must constantly re-adapt because the distribution of its inputs keeps changing as the preceding layers are updated. By normalizing inputs to each layer, BN supposedly reduces this shift.

This narrative is compelling but contested. Santurkar et al. (2018) showed that BatchNorm doesn't actually reduce covariate shift measurably. Instead, they demonstrated that BatchNorm's real benefit is smoothing the loss landscape — making the optimization surface more Lipschitz-continuous, which allows larger learning rates and faster convergence.

Don't get hung up on covariate shift. The original motivation is likely wrong, but the technique unquestionably works. The real story is about loss landscape geometry and gradient behavior.

Formalism 3.2 — The Mathematics of Normalization

3.2.1 Batch Normalization vs Layer Normalization

The fundamental difference between normalization techniques comes down to which dimensions you compute statistics over. Consider a typical Transformer input tensor with shape (batch, seq_len, d_model) — three axes, three possible normalization strategies.

Figure 3.2. Given tensor shape (batch, seq_len, d_model): BatchNorm computes statistics across the batch+sequence axes (vertical blue slices), while LayerNorm computes across the feature axis (horizontal green rows).

Why BatchNorm Fails for Sequences

BatchNorm was designed for convolutional networks with fixed spatial dimensions. In NLP and Transformer models, it breaks down for three critical reasons:

  1. Variable-length sequences. Positions beyond the length of shorter sequences in the batch are padding tokens. Computing batch statistics over these positions produces meaningless numbers.
  2. Train-vs-inference mismatch. BatchNorm maintains running statistics during training but uses fixed statistics at inference. For sequences, these running statistics are unreliable because the batch composition varies wildly.
  3. Batch dependency. Each example's normalization depends on what other examples are in the batch. This is undesirable: the representation of "The cat sat" shouldn't change depending on whether the other sentences in the batch are about cats or about astrophysics.

LayerNorm solves all three problems by normalizing each individual token's feature vector independently — no dependence on other examples, no train/inference discrepancy, no issues with variable lengths.

Property Batch Normalization Layer Normalization
Normalization axis Batch + spatial/sequence Feature dimension only
Statistics per Per feature channel Per example (per token)
Batch dependency Yes — requires large batches No — fully independent
Train/inference difference Yes — running statistics at inference No — identical at train and inference
Variable-length sequences Problematic (padding) No issue
Primary domain CNNs (vision) Transformers (NLP, vision, etc.)
Learnable params \(\gamma, \beta\) per feature \(\gamma, \beta\) per feature

3.2.2 Layer Normalization: Math and Properties

Given an input vector \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}\) representing a single token, LayerNorm computes:

\[ \mu = \frac{1}{d} \sum_{i=1}^{d} x_i \]
\[ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d} \sum_{i=1}^{d} (x_i - \mu)^2} \]
\[ \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \boldsymbol{\gamma} \odot \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sigma + \epsilon} + \boldsymbol{\beta} \]

where:

  • \(\mu\) is the mean of the feature vector (a scalar, computed per token)
  • \(\sigma\) is the standard deviation of the feature vector (a scalar, per token)
  • \(\boldsymbol{\gamma} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\) is a learnable scale parameter (initialized to ones)
  • \(\boldsymbol{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\) is a learnable shift parameter (initialized to zeros)
  • \(\odot\) denotes element-wise (Hadamard) multiplication
  • \(\epsilon\) is a small constant (typically \(10^{-6}\)) for numerical stability
Key intuition: The bare normalization \((\mathbf{x} - \mu)/\sigma\) forces every token to have mean 0 and variance 1. The learnable \(\gamma\) and \(\beta\) let the network undo this constraint where useful — giving the model the option of normalization without mandating any particular output distribution.

Critically, all statistics are computed within a single token's feature vector. Token at position 5 is normalized using only its own 512 (or 768, or 4096) feature values — completely independent of all other tokens and all other examples in the batch.

3.2.3 Post-LN: The Original Transformer Placement

In the original "Attention Is All You Need" paper (Vaswani et al., 2017), LayerNorm is placed after the residual addition:

\[ \text{output} = \text{LayerNorm}\!\left(\mathbf{x} + \text{Sublayer}(\mathbf{x})\right) \]

This is called Post-LN because normalization happens post residual connection. Let's visualize the block structure:

Figure 3.3. Post-LN places LayerNorm after the residual addition; Pre-LN places it before the sublayer. The gradient magnitude plot (bottom) shows Pre-LN produces well-bounded gradients, while Post-LN gradients vary wildly (Xiong et al., 2020).

The Post-LN Gradient Problem

Xiong et al. (2020) — "On Layer Normalization in the Transformer Architecture" — provided a rigorous analysis showing that Post-LN has problematic gradient behavior at initialization:

  • Gradients at early layers can be orders of magnitude larger than gradients at later layers
  • This imbalance means early layers get disproportionately large updates, potentially causing divergence
  • A learning rate warmup is essential: start with a tiny learning rate and gradually increase it, allowing the model to reach a more stable region before taking large steps
  • Without warmup, Post-LN training frequently diverges entirely

3.2.4 Pre-LN: The Modern Standard

Pre-LN moves LayerNorm to before the sublayer:

\[ \text{output} = \mathbf{x} + \text{Sublayer}\!\left(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{x})\right) \]

This seemingly trivial rearrangement has a profound consequence for gradient flow. Consider what happens when we compute the gradient of the loss with respect to an input at layer \(\ell\):

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{x}_\ell} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{x}_L} \prod_{k=\ell}^{L-1} \left(\mathbf{I} + \frac{\partial\, \text{Sublayer}_k(\text{LN}(\mathbf{x}_k))}{\partial \mathbf{x}_k}\right) \]

The identity matrix \(\mathbf{I}\) in each factor ensures that the gradient always has a direct path — the residual connection acts as a "gradient highway" that is not gated by any normalization. In Post-LN, the LayerNorm sits in this path and can distort the gradient.

This simple change — moving LayerNorm before the sublayer instead of after — was one of the most impactful practical improvements to the Transformer architecture. It enabled training deep Transformers (48+ layers) without careful learning rate warmup schedules and opened the door to the massive models we see today.

Properties of Pre-LN

  • Well-bounded gradients at initialization — Xiong et al. proved this formally
  • No warmup needed — training can begin with the full learning rate
  • Clean residual path — the residual stream is an unobstructed identity mapping
  • Easier to scale — deeper models train more reliably

Where Pre-LN is Used

Pre-LN is the standard in virtually all modern large language models:

  • GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) — first major model to adopt Pre-LN
  • GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020)
  • LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) and LLaMA 2/3
  • PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2022)
  • Mistral, Gemma, and essentially all open-weight LLMs

The Trade-Off

There is some evidence (Liu et al., 2020) that Post-LN can achieve slightly better final loss than Pre-LN when trained successfully. The hypothesis is that normalization after the residual provides a stronger regularization effect. However, the training difficulty of Post-LN makes this advantage impractical for most settings, especially at scale.

3.2.5 RMSNorm: Dropping the Mean

Zhang & Sennrich (2019) observed that the mean-centering step in LayerNorm may be redundant. Their insight: the re-centering (\(x - \mu\)) and the re-scaling (\(/\sigma\)) serve partially overlapping roles. What if we just normalize by the root-mean-square of the values?

\[ \text{RMS}(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d} x_i^2} \]
\[ \text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \boldsymbol{\gamma} \odot \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\text{RMS}(\mathbf{x}) + \epsilon} \]

Compared to LayerNorm:

  • No mean subtraction — we skip computing \(\mu\) entirely
  • No shift parameter \(\beta\) — only the scale \(\gamma\) remains
  • ~10-15% faster than LayerNorm due to fewer operations
  • Fewer parameters — \(d\) instead of \(2d\) learnable values per norm layer

The empirical result from Zhang & Sennrich: RMSNorm performs equally well as LayerNorm across a variety of tasks, while being computationally cheaper. This is a pure win when training billion-parameter models where every saved FLOP matters.

Why does dropping the mean work? One hypothesis: after many layers of learned transformations, the mean of feature vectors carries relatively little useful information compared to the magnitudes. The scale normalization alone is sufficient to keep activations well-behaved.

RMSNorm in Production

RMSNorm has become the default in the latest generation of LLMs:

  • LLaMA and LLaMA 2/3 (Meta)
  • Gemma (Google DeepMind)
  • Mistral and Mixtral
  • Qwen (Alibaba)

Engineering 3.3 — Implementations

3.3.1 Implementing LayerNorm from Scratch

A clean, readable implementation that matches PyTorch's nn.LayerNorm behavior. The key is normalizing over the last dimension (dim=-1), which is the feature/embedding dimension. 

import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class LayerNorm(nn.Module):
    """Layer Normalization (Ba et al., 2016).

    Normalizes each token's feature vector to have zero mean and unit
    variance, then applies a learnable affine transformation.

    Args:
        d_model: Dimension of the feature vector (e.g., 512, 768, 4096).
        eps: Small constant for numerical stability. Default: 1e-6.
    """

    def __init__(self, d_model: int, eps: float = 1e-6):
        super().__init__()
        self.gamma = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(d_model))   # scale
        self.beta = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(d_model))    # shift
        self.eps = eps

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        """
        Args:
            x: Input tensor of shape (..., d_model).
        Returns:
            Normalized tensor of the same shape.
        """
        # Compute mean and std over the last (feature) dimension
        mean = x.mean(dim=-1, keepdim=True)          # (..., 1)
        std = x.std(dim=-1, keepdim=True)             # (..., 1)

        # Normalize, then scale and shift
        x_norm = (x - mean) / (std + self.eps)        # (..., d_model)
        return self.gamma * x_norm + self.beta         # (..., d_model)


# --- Quick test ---
if __name__ == "__main__":
    ln = LayerNorm(d_model=512)
    x = torch.randn(2, 10, 512)   # (batch=2, seq_len=10, d_model=512)
    out = ln(x)
    print(f"Input shape:  {x.shape}")
    print(f"Output shape: {out.shape}")
    # Verify: each token should have ~0 mean and ~1 std after normalization
    normed = (x - x.mean(-1, keepdim=True)) / (x.std(-1, keepdim=True) + 1e-6)
    print(f"Post-norm mean: {normed.mean(-1)[0, 0]:.6f}")   # ~0
    print(f"Post-norm std:  {normed.std(-1)[0, 0]:.6f}")    # ~1

3.3.2 Implementing RMSNorm from Scratch

RMSNorm is even simpler: no mean computation, no shift parameter. Note the difference — we compute the root-mean-square directly from \(x^2\) rather than from \((x - \mu)^2\). 

class RMSNorm(nn.Module):
    """Root Mean Square Layer Normalization (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019).

    Like LayerNorm but without mean-centering. Normalizes by the RMS of
    the input and applies a learnable scale. Faster and equally effective.

    Args:
        d_model: Dimension of the feature vector.
        eps: Small constant for numerical stability. Default: 1e-6.
    """

    def __init__(self, d_model: int, eps: float = 1e-6):
        super().__init__()
        self.gamma = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(d_model))   # scale only, no shift
        self.eps = eps

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        """
        Args:
            x: Input tensor of shape (..., d_model).
        Returns:
            Normalized tensor of the same shape.
        """
        # RMS = sqrt(mean(x^2))
        rms = torch.sqrt(
            torch.mean(x ** 2, dim=-1, keepdim=True) + self.eps
        )
        return self.gamma * (x / rms)


# --- Quick test ---
if __name__ == "__main__":
    rms_norm = RMSNorm(d_model=512)
    x = torch.randn(2, 10, 512)
    out = rms_norm(x)
    print(f"Input shape:  {x.shape}")
    print(f"Output shape: {out.shape}")
    # Verify: output RMS should be close to 1 (before gamma scaling)
    out_rms = torch.sqrt(torch.mean(out ** 2, dim=-1))
    print(f"Output RMS (should be ~1): {out_rms[0, 0]:.4f}")

3.3.3 Pre-LN vs Post-LN Transformer Blocks

Below are complete implementations of both block variants. Notice how the only difference is the position of the normalization call — the rest of the architecture is identical.

Post-LN Block (Original)

class PostLNTransformerBlock(nn.Module):
    """Transformer block with Post-LN placement.

    LN(x + Sublayer(x)) — normalization AFTER residual.
    Requires learning rate warmup for stable training.
    """

    def __init__(self, d_model, n_heads, d_ff, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        # Sublayers
        self.attn = MultiHeadAttention(d_model, n_heads)
        self.ffn = FeedForward(d_model, d_ff)

        # Normalization AFTER residual (Post-LN)
        self.norm1 = LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.norm2 = LayerNorm(d_model)

        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)

    def forward(self, x, mask=None):
        # Step 1: Attention + Add + Norm
        attn_out = self.attn(x, x, x, mask)
        x = self.norm1(x + self.dropout(attn_out))
        #    ^^^^^ LN wraps the sum

        # Step 2: FFN + Add + Norm
        ffn_out = self.ffn(x)
        x = self.norm2(x + self.dropout(ffn_out))
        #    ^^^^^ LN wraps the sum

        return x

Pre-LN Block (Modern)

class PreLNTransformerBlock(nn.Module):
    """Transformer block with Pre-LN placement.

    x + Sublayer(LN(x)) — normalization BEFORE sublayer.
    Stable training without warmup. Used in GPT-2/3, LLaMA.
    """

    def __init__(self, d_model, n_heads, d_ff, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        # Sublayers
        self.attn = MultiHeadAttention(d_model, n_heads)
        self.ffn = FeedForward(d_model, d_ff)

        # Normalization BEFORE sublayer (Pre-LN)
        self.norm1 = LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.norm2 = LayerNorm(d_model)

        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)

    def forward(self, x, mask=None):
        # Step 1: Norm + Attention + Add
        normed = self.norm1(x)
        attn_out = self.attn(normed, normed, normed, mask)
        x = x + self.dropout(attn_out)
        #   ^ clean residual addition

        # Step 2: Norm + FFN + Add
        normed = self.norm2(x)
        ffn_out = self.ffn(normed)
        x = x + self.dropout(ffn_out)
        #   ^ clean residual addition

        return x
Spot the pattern: In Post-LN, normalization wraps the residual sum — norm(x + sublayer(x)). In Pre-LN, normalization feeds into the sublayer, and the residual addition happens last — x + sublayer(norm(x)). This keeps the residual path clean in Pre-LN.

3.3.4 Numerical Stability in Mixed Precision

Modern training uses mixed precision (FP16 or BF16) for speed. But normalization involves computing means, variances, and divisions — operations that are highly sensitive to numerical precision. In FP16, the representable range is approximately \(\pm 65{,}504\), and values like \(x^2\) can easily overflow.

The standard solution: upcast to FP32 for the normalization computation, then cast back. 

class RMSNormFP32(nn.Module):
    """RMSNorm with safe mixed-precision computation.

    Upcasts to FP32 for the normalization math, then casts back
    to the input dtype. This prevents overflow in FP16/BF16 training.
    """

    def __init__(self, d_model: int, eps: float = 1e-6):
        super().__init__()
        self.gamma = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(d_model))
        self.eps = eps

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        input_dtype = x.dtype

        # Upcast to FP32 for safe computation
        x_fp32 = x.float()

        # Compute RMS in FP32 — no overflow risk
        rms = torch.sqrt(
            torch.mean(x_fp32 ** 2, dim=-1, keepdim=True) + self.eps
        )
        x_normed = x_fp32 / rms

        # Apply scale (also in FP32), then cast back
        output = self.gamma.float() * x_normed
        return output.to(input_dtype)


# This pattern is used in HuggingFace Transformers, LLaMA, etc.
# Example from LLaMA's implementation:
#   variance = hidden_states.to(torch.float32).pow(2).mean(-1, keepdim=True)
#   hidden_states = hidden_states * torch.rsqrt(variance + self.eps)
#   return self.weight * hidden_states.to(input_dtype)
Performance note: The upcast to FP32 adds minimal overhead because the normalization computation is memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. The few extra FLOPS from FP32 arithmetic are negligible compared to the memory traffic of reading and writing the tensor.

3.4 Going Deeper: Advanced Normalization Topics

QK-Norm: Taming Attention Logits

As models grow wider (\(d_{\text{model}}\) increases), the dot products \(\mathbf{q}^\top \mathbf{k}\) in attention can grow large even after the \(\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling. QK-Norm applies LayerNorm (or RMSNorm) to the query and key vectors before the dot product:

\[ \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\!\left( \frac{\text{Norm}(\mathbf{Q}) \cdot \text{Norm}(\mathbf{K})^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} \right) \mathbf{V} \]

This prevents attention logit growth during training and is used in models like ViT-22B and Gemma 2. It's especially important for very long context lengths where attention scores can become extreme.

DeepNorm: Scaling to 1000+ Layers

Wang et al. (2022) proposed DeepNorm for training extremely deep Transformers (up to 1000 layers). The idea: scale the residual connection by a constant \(\alpha > 1\) and scale the sublayer initialization by \(\beta < 1\):

\[ \text{output} = \text{LayerNorm}\!\left(\alpha \cdot \mathbf{x} + \text{Sublayer}(\mathbf{x})\right) \]

The scaling constants depend on the model depth \(N\): \(\alpha = (2N)^{1/4}\), and Xavier initialization is scaled by \(\beta = (8N)^{-1/4}\). This creates a strong "residual bias" that stabilizes gradient flow in extreme-depth regimes.

The Bigger Question: Is Normalization Fundamental?

Some researchers question whether normalization is truly necessary or merely a patch for poor initialization and optimization:

  • Fixup Initialization (Zhang et al., 2019) showed that carefully designed initialization can replace normalization in ResNets.
  • NF-Nets (Brock et al., 2021) achieved state-of-the-art ImageNet performance without any normalization layers.
  • For Transformers, however, no one has yet demonstrated competitive performance without normalization at scale. The interaction between attention, residual connections, and normalization appears to be deeply intertwined.

Open question: will future architectures find a way to eliminate normalization entirely, or is it a fundamental ingredient of stable deep learning?
