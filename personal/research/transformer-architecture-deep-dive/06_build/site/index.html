0.1   Why Transformers Changed Everything

The Pre-Transformer Era

Before June 2017, when Vaswani et al. published "Attention Is All You Need", the dominant paradigm for processing sequences—text, speech, time series—was the recurrent neural network (RNN) and its gated cousins, the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network and the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU). These architectures shared a fundamental design choice: they processed sequences one token at a time, maintaining a hidden state $h_t$ that was updated at each timestep:

$$h_t = f(h_{t-1}, x_t)$$

This sequential dependency was both their strength and their Achilles' heel. On the one hand, the recurrent structure naturally captured the ordering of tokens. On the other hand, it imposed three severe constraints:

  1. Sequential computation. Because $h_t$ depends on $h_{t-1}$, you cannot compute the hidden state for position 50 until you have computed positions 1 through 49. This makes RNNs fundamentally non-parallelizable, which is catastrophic on modern GPU hardware designed for massively parallel operations.
  2. Vanishing gradients. Despite the gating mechanisms in LSTMs, information from distant positions still decays as it is passed through dozens or hundreds of sequential update steps. A word at position 5 has a hard time influencing the representation at position 500.
  3. Fixed-size bottleneck. In encoder-decoder models (used for machine translation), the entire source sentence was compressed into a single fixed-dimensional vector $h_T$—the final hidden state. This bottleneck limited the model's ability to faithfully represent long or complex inputs.

The Attention Revolution

The first crack in the RNN monopoly appeared in 2014-2015, when Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio introduced the attention mechanism as an add-on to the encoder-decoder RNN. Instead of forcing the decoder to rely solely on the final encoder hidden state, attention allowed the decoder to look back at all encoder hidden states and compute a weighted combination:

$$\text{context}_t = \sum_{i=1}^{T} \alpha_{t,i} \cdot h_i^{\text{enc}}$$

where $\alpha_{t,i}$ is the attention weight indicating how relevant encoder position $i$ is to the current decoder position $t$. This was a revolution: the model could now directly access any position in the input, bypassing the sequential bottleneck.

But Bahdanau attention was still attached to an RNN. The encoder and decoder were still recurrent. Attention was a supplement, not a replacement. The insight of the Transformer was radical in its simplicity:

Key Insight

The key insight of the Transformer is that attention alone—without recurrence—is sufficient for building powerful sequence models. By replacing all recurrent layers with self-attention layers and adding positional encodings to preserve order information, the Transformer achieves superior performance while being fully parallelizable.

This was not merely an incremental improvement. It was an architectural paradigm shift. By removing the sequential dependency, the Transformer could process all positions in a sequence simultaneously. A 512-token sequence that would require 512 sequential steps in an RNN could now be processed in a single forward pass through an attention layer. The result was dramatic speedups in training and, more importantly, the ability to scale to much larger models and datasets.

The consequences have been extraordinary. Within seven years of the original paper, Transformer-based models—GPT, BERT, T5, PaLM, LLaMA, Claude, and many others—have come to dominate not just NLP, but also computer vision (ViT), protein structure prediction (AlphaFold), code generation (Codex), and reinforcement learning (Decision Transformer). It is no exaggeration to say that the Transformer is the most influential neural network architecture since the invention of backpropagation.

Historical Timeline

Notice the inflection point at 2017. Before the Transformer, progress in NLP was steady but incremental. After it, the field entered an exponential growth phase: BERT and GPT arrived within a year, scaling laws were discovered, and within five years, language models were writing code, passing medical exams, and holding coherent multi-turn conversations. Understanding the Transformer architecture is not optional for anyone working in modern AI—it is the foundation upon which everything else is built.

0.2   Architecture Overview

The Transformer follows an encoder-decoder structure, though many modern variants use only the encoder (BERT) or only the decoder (GPT). The full architecture consists of stacked layers, each containing self-attention and feed-forward sub-layers, connected by residual connections and layer normalization. Let us examine the complete picture.

0.3   How a Transformer Processes Text

To build intuition for the Transformer, let us trace a complete forward pass from raw text to output probabilities. We will follow the sentence "The cat sat" through every stage of the pipeline.

The Forward Pass, Step by Step

1

Tokenization

The raw string is split into tokens by the tokenizer (e.g., BPE, SentencePiece, or WordPiece). Each token is mapped to an integer ID from the vocabulary:

"The cat sat" → [The, cat, sat] → [1024, 3782, 892]

Modern tokenizers operate at the subword level. The word "unhappiness" might become [un, happi, ness]. This provides a good balance between vocabulary size and representation granularity. A typical vocabulary contains 32,000–100,000 tokens.

2

Token Embedding

Each token ID is converted into a dense vector of dimension $d_{\text{model}}$ (typically 512, 768, or 4096) by looking up a learned embedding matrix $W_E \in \mathbb{R}^{|V| \times d_{\text{model}}}$:

$\mathbf{x}_i = W_E[\text{token}_i] \quad \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$

These embeddings are learned end-to-end during training. The embedding vectors encode rich semantic information: words with similar meanings end up as nearby vectors in embedding space. Following the original paper, the embeddings are scaled by $\sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$ to keep their magnitude balanced with the positional encodings.

3

Positional Encoding

Because self-attention treats its input as a set (it is permutation-equivariant), the model has no inherent notion of word order. Positional encodings are added to the embeddings to inject position information. The original Transformer used sinusoidal functions:

$PE_{(pos, 2i)} = \sin\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$,   $PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} = \cos\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$

The final input to the encoder is: $\mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{PE}_i$. Modern models often use learned positional encodings or rotary position embeddings (RoPE).

Positional Encoding →

4

N Encoder Layers

The position-encoded vectors are passed through $N$ identical encoder layers (the original paper used $N = 6$). Each layer contains two sub-layers:

(a) Multi-head self-attention: Each token computes attention scores with all other tokens, creating a weighted sum that captures contextual relationships. With 8 attention heads, the model can attend to different types of relationships simultaneously (syntactic, semantic, positional, etc.). Self-Attention →

(b) Position-wise feed-forward network: A two-layer MLP with a wider hidden layer (typically $4 \times d_{\text{model}}$) applied independently to each position. This is where much of the model's "knowledge" is stored. FFN & Activations →

Each sub-layer uses a residual connection followed by layer normalization: $\text{output} = \text{LayerNorm}(x + \text{Sublayer}(x))$. Normalization →

5

Output Projection

After the final encoder/decoder layer, the representations are projected to the vocabulary size through a linear layer $W_O \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times |V|}$ (often sharing weights with the input embedding matrix). A softmax produces a probability distribution over the vocabulary:

$P(\text{next token}) = \text{softmax}(W_O \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{final}})$

During training, this distribution is compared to the true next token using cross-entropy loss. During inference, we sample from (or take the argmax of) this distribution to generate the next token. Training →

Pseudocode: The Complete Forward Pass

The following pseudocode captures the essence of a Transformer encoder forward pass in roughly 10 lines. Notice the elegant simplicity: it is just embeddings, followed by repeated applications of attention and feed-forward sub-layers, each wrapped in residual connections and layer normalization. 

def transformer_forward(tokens):
    """Complete forward pass through a Transformer encoder."""
    # Step 1-3: Embed tokens and add positional information
    x = embedding(tokens) + positional_encoding(positions)

    # Step 4: Pass through N identical encoder layers
    for layer in encoder_layers:
        # Self-attention sub-layer with residual connection
        x = x + attention(layer_norm(x))    # self-attention + residual
        # Feed-forward sub-layer with residual connection
        x = x + ffn(layer_norm(x))          # feed-forward + residual

    # Step 5: Project to vocabulary and compute probabilities
    logits = output_projection(layer_norm(x))
    return softmax(logits)
Notation Note

The pseudocode above uses Pre-LN (pre-normalization) ordering, where layer_norm is applied before each sub-layer. This is the standard in modern Transformers (GPT-2 onwards) and differs from the original paper's Post-LN ordering, where normalization was applied after the residual addition. Pre-LN is more stable during training and enables the training of deeper models without warmup tricks. See the Normalization → page for a detailed comparison.

The Residual Stream Interpretation

A powerful way to think about the Transformer is through the residual stream metaphor, popularized by the mechanistic interpretability community (Elhage et al., 2021). Instead of viewing each layer as a sequential transformation, imagine a shared information highway—the residual stream—that flows from the embedding layer to the output.

Each sub-layer (attention or FFN) reads from the residual stream, performs some computation, and writes its result back to the stream via the residual connection. The key insight is that all layers are operating on the same shared representation, not building on top of each other's outputs:

This residual stream view has several important implications:

  • Superposition: The residual stream carries information for many different "features" simultaneously, superimposed in the same vector space. Different attention heads and FFN neurons can independently read and write specific features.
  • Skip connections enable depth: Because each sub-layer only needs to learn the delta (what to add to the stream), gradients flow cleanly through the identity connection even in very deep models (96+ layers).
  • Early layers = local syntax, later layers = global semantics: Research has shown that early Transformer layers tend to encode syntactic relationships (subject-verb agreement, dependency parsing), while later layers encode higher-level semantic content (sentiment, topic, factual knowledge).
Key Idea

Think of the residual stream as a shared whiteboard. Each attention head and FFN layer is a specialist that reads relevant information from the whiteboard, performs its computation, and writes the result back. The final output is the cumulative result of all these specialists contributing to the same shared workspace.

It is useful to keep the key dimensions in mind as we study the architecture. Here are the standard sizes from the original "base" and "big" Transformer configurations:

Notice that the head dimension $d_k$ is always $d_{\text{model}} / h$. This means the total computation of multi-head attention is the same as single-head attention with full dimension—multi-head attention does not add computation, it restructures it into parallel, lower-dimensional subspaces. This is a subtle but crucial design choice.
